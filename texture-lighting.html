Introduction to Texture

Texture involves projecting or wrapping 2D images onto 3D objects to efficiently simulate surface details and attributes, such as reflectivity and roughness, without increasing the geometric polygon count, whereas lighting is the process that reveals the 3D world and establishes the scene's mood.

Textures are used in 3D rendering as efficient shortcuts to simulate complex surface details and modulate attributes such as reflectivity, transparency, and roughness, allowing for the creation of rich visual effects without the computational cost of adding extra polygons.

Lighting serves the fundamental purpose of revealing the 3D world by making objects visible and defining their depth and shape through the strategic placement of highlights and shadows. Beyond basic visibility, it establishes the emotional tone or mood of a scene.

Fundamentals

Texture Map Explained

Essential surfacing components. Click images to view full screen.

Color/Pattern Maps

Flat simulations that act like "wallpaper," providing surface details that look textured but remain smooth to the touch.

Transparency Maps

Grayscale images determining opaque or transparent parts. Using Alpha channels to "cut holes" without extra geometry.

Bump Maps

Simulates wrinkles by disturbing the surface normal, creating a relief illusion through lighting.

Displacement Maps

Unlike bump maps, these actually modify the geometry. Used for 3D terrain where elevations are coded in grayscale.

Spherical Maps

A flattened environment image mapped inside a virtual sphere for realistic reflections.

Cubical (Box) Maps

A six-panel environment view mapped onto a virtual cube for efficient reflections.
